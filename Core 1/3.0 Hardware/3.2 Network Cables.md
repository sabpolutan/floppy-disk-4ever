## The importance of cable
- **Fundamental to network communication**
	- Incredibly important foundation
- **Usually, you only get one good opportunity at building your cabling infrastructure**
	- Make it good!
	- If you have bad cabling, then your applications are not going to work as expected.
	- Plan and install the correct cabling for the type of network that you plan to use.
- **The vast majority of wireless communication uses cables**
	- It has to plug in somewhere

# Twisted pair copper cabling
- They make up a huge number of cables that we use on wired Ethernet networks
- **Balanced pair operation**
	- Two wires with equal and opposite signals
	- `Transmit+`, `Transmit-` / `Receive+`, `Receive-`
- **The twist is the secret!**
	- Keeps a single wire constantly moving away from the interference
	- The opposite signals are compared on the other end
- **Pairs in the same cable have different twist rates**
	- The receiving side can compare all of the wire pairs and see the differences in that interference across all of those pairs at the same time.
	- Since there are differences in the twist rate, the receiving device can easily see where the interference might be and where the good signal might be inside that cable.

## Cable categories
- **Cables don't have speed**
	- The copper just sits there
- **Electrical signals are sent over copper cable**
	- The signal encoding determines the data transfer rate
- **A cable must be manufactured to specific standards**
	- *IEEE 802.3 Ethernet standards* determine the cable type
- **Cable standards are described as a "category" of cable**
	- Category 5e, Category 6A, etc.
	- Check the *IEEE standard* to determine the minimum cable category
	- The minimum cable category for *1000BASE-T* is Category 5 (now deprecated)

## Coaxial cables
- **Two or more forms sharing a common axis**
	- The inner conductor and the outer shield
- **RG-6 used in television / digital cable**
	- And high-speed Internet over cable

## Unshielded and shielded cable
- **UTP (Unshielded Twisted Pair)**
	- No additional shielding
	- The most common twisted pair cabling
- **STP (Shielded Twisted Pair)**
	- Additional shielding protects against interference
	- Shield each pair and/ or the overall cable
	- Requires the cable to be grounded
- **Abbreviations**
	- *U* = Unshielded
	- *S* = Braided shielding
	- *F* = Foil shielding
- `(Overall cable)` / `(individual pairs)TP`
	- Braided shielding around the entire cable and foil around the pairs is $S/FTP$
	- Foil around the cable and no shielding around the pairs is $F/UTP$

## Direct burial STP
- Typically used by an organization of multiple buildings, or a campus of buildings
- Some organizations might run wires overhead between one building and another
- **Overhead cable isn't always a good option**
	- Put the cable in the ground for better protection
- **Direct burial STP provides protection from the elements**
	- Designed to be waterproof
	- Often filled with gel to repel water
	- Conduit may not be needed
- **Usually shielded twisted pair**
	- Provides grounding
	- Adds strength
	- Protects against signal interference

## Plenum
### No plenum
![](<./assets/3.2 Network Cables/file-20260304192323893.png>)[^3]
- There are additional things on the ceiling that you cannot see.
- Above the drop ceiling might be some ductwork that is able to feed air into the rest of the building,
	- and another set of ductwork that is able to take a return air supply and send it back to the air conditioning system.
- When the airspace above that drop ceiling is not circulating, it is referred as not having a *plenum*.

### Plenum space
![](<./assets/3.2 Network Cables/file-20260304192817648.png>)[^4]
- In many commercial environments, there is often an air supply that provides fresh air through a set of ductwork.
- The return air is simply going into the large, open air space above the drop ceiling.
	- The open area is referred to as "plenum space".
- **Plenum space**
	- Building air circulation
	- Heating and air conditioning system
- **Concerns in the case of fire**
	- Vulnerable to fire, smoke, and toxic fumes
- **Worst-case planning**
	- Important concerns for any structure
	- If you're putting network cables above the drop ceiling, you have to use a cable type that has been specifically built for a plenum.


### Plenum-rated cable
- **Traditional cable jacket**
	- Polyvinyl chloride (PVC)
- **Fire-rated cable jacket**
	- Fluorinated ethylene polymer (FEP)
	- Low-smoke polyvinyl chloride (PVC)
- **Plenum-rated cable may not be as flexible**
	- They are functionally the same as any other type of Ethernet cable
		- However, they can be difficult to work with
	- May not have the same bend radius
- **Worst-case planning**
	- Important concerns for any structure
	- If you're designing a network of cables above a drop ceiling, make sure you're using a plenum-rated cable.
