## Fiber communication
- **Transmission by light**
	- The visible spectrum
	- Instead of using copper with electrical signals to transmit information from point A to point B, we send light through a fiber to transmit the same information instead.
- **No RF signal**[^1]
	- Very difficult to monitor or tap into the connection.
		- Requires specialized equipment if you're planning to use a fiber optic network.
	- We don't have to worry about the interference from other equipment or radio frequency devices.
- **Signal is slow to degrade**
	- Transmission over long distances
- **Immune to radio interference**
	- There's no RF

### Fiber Optic Cable Cross-section Diagram
#### **Core Components & Data Transmission**
- **LED (Source):** Acts as the transmitter that converts electrical signals into light rays to enter the cable.
	- In some cases, we use a laser to be able to transmit that light.
- **Light Ray:** The data travels as a beam of light that bounces along the inside of the cable.
- **Receiver:** Located at the opposite end, this component captures the light rays and converts them back into usable data.
#### **Internal Cable Structure**
The cable is composed of three primary layers, each with a specific physical property:
- **Core:** *The innermost layer where the light actually travels.*
    - It possesses a **High Reflective Index**, which ensures the light stays trapped inside through a process called total internal reflection.
- **Cladding:** *The middle layer surrounding the core.*
    - It has a **Low Reflective Index**, which causes the light rays to "bounce" back into the core rather than escaping through the sides of the cable.
    - It helps protect the High Reflective Index core.
- **Buffer Coating:** *The outermost layer that is shown in dark purple.*
    - Its primary purpose is **Mechanical Protection**, shielding the delicate glass or plastic inner layers from physical damage, moisture, and bending stress.
    - The fiber is very thin and very fragile.
#### **Performance Highlight**
- **Speed:** The large yellow arrow at the bottom emphasizes that the primary advantage of this specific construction is the high-speed transmission of data over long distances.


## Multi-mode fiber
![](<./assets/3.2 Optical Fiber/file-20260306164010175.png>)[^3]
- **This type of fiber is relatively large**
	- Has different modes it can take as it moves through the fiber
		- Each time the light goes through, it may use a different path to get from one side of the fiber to the other.
- **Short-range communication**
	- Up to 2 km in length
- **Relatively inexpensive light source**
	- i.e., LED

## Single-mode fiber
![](<./assets/3.2 Optical Fiber/file-20260306164108441.png>)[^4]
- **Long-range communication** 
	- Up to 100 km without processing
- **Expensive light source**
	- Commonly uses lasers
- The core of the fiber is much narrower than a multi-mode fiber
	- Single path through the entire single-mode connection.
