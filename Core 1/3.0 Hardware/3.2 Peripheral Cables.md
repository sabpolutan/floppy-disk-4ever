## USB (Universal Serial Bus)
![569](<./assets/3.2 Peripheral Cables/file-20260306170332256.png>)[^1]
- **Simplify connections**
	- Printers, storage devices, keyboard, mouse
- **USB 1.1**
	- One of the first versions of USB
	- *Low speed*: 1.5 megabits per second, 3 meters
	- *Full speed*: 12 megabits per second, 5 meters
- **USB 2.0**
	- 480 megabits per second, 5 meters
- **USB 3.0**
	- Superspeed
	- 5 gigabits per second, ~3 meters[^2]
	- The USB specification/ standard does not specify an exact cable length
- **USB 3.1**
	- A maximum of 10 gigabits per second
- **USB 3.2**
	- A maximum of 20 gigabits per second

![506](<./assets/3.2 Peripheral Cables/file-20260306170645202.png>)[^3]

## USB-C
![286](<./assets/3.2 Peripheral Cables/file-20260306171638301.png>)[^4]
- **USB has a lot of different connectors**
	- And they have changed over time
- **Can be annoying to connect a USB-A**
	- Third time's the charm
- **USB-C replaces all of these**
	- One connector to rule them all
	- Can effectively replace all of the other USB connector types
- **USB-C describes the physical connector**
	- It doesn't describe the type of signal
	- There could be many different kinds of signal that you're putting through a cable that is ultimately connected with a USB-C connector.

![678](<./assets/3.2 Peripheral Cables/file-20260306171324590.png>)[^5]

## Serial cables
- **D-subminiature or D-sub**
	- The letter refers to the connector size
![445](<./assets/3.2 Peripheral Cables/file-20260306172135465.png>)[^6]
![444](<./assets/3.2 Peripheral Cables/file-20260306172344753.png>)[^7]

- **The most popular were the *DB-9* and the *DB-25***
	- The size of *DB-9* refers to the size of the connector
	- The *number* refers to the number of pins
		- DB-25 has 25 pins
		- DB-9 has 9 pins
	- The *B* usually refers to a larger size connector, whereas a smaller connector is referred to as an *E-size* connector
		- Instead of *DB-9*, it would be technically referred to as *DE-9*
			- Older serial cables may refer to this as both
			- *DE-9* is the more technically correct designated name
- **Commonly used for RS-232**
	- **R**ecommended **S**tandard **232**
	- An industry standard for sending serial information over these wires since 1969
- **Serial communication standard**
	- Built for modem communication
	- Used for modems, printers, mice, and networking
- **Now used as a configuration port**
	- DB-9 connectors being used today would be connected to what is now *legacy* equipment
		- Older switch or an older router
		- Allows you to connect to the serial or console port on these devices to be able to configure them.
![526](<./assets/3.2 Peripheral Cables/computer-port-types-and-connections-vector-1213929.jpg>)[^8]
- **Directly connect to the device**
	- Traditionally a serial connection
	- DB9 connector, RJ45 serial, USB connection
- **When all else fails**
	- The console will be available through a serial interface
	- Best method to connect to a device when no other method is available
- **The text-based serial interface**
	- The console
- **Requires a serial or USB connection**
	- For modern devices
	- May need a USB to DB9 serial adapter

## Thunderbolt
![](<./assets/3.2 Peripheral Cables/file-20260306175513992.png>)[^9]
- **High-speed serial connector**
	- Data and power on the same cable
	- Based on the Mini DisplayPort (MDP) standard
- **Thunderbolt 1**
	- Two channels
	- 10 Gbit/s per channel
	- 20 Gbit/s total throughput
	- Mini DisplayPort connector
- **Thunderbolt 2**
	- 20 Gbit/s aggregated channels
	- Mini DisplayPort connector
![](<./assets/3.2 Peripheral Cables/file-20260306175438771.png>)[^10]
- **Thunderbolt 3**
	- 40 Gbit/s aggregated throughput
	- USB-C connector
- **Maximum 3 meters (copper)**
	- 60 meters (optical)
	- Daisy-chain up to 6 devices
- **Thunderbolt 4**
	- Still 40 Gbit/s aggregated throughput
	- Support for dual 4K displays
	- Increased PCIe (PCI Express bus) bandwidth
		- This allows much more data to be transferred from the motherboard to the peripheral devices
